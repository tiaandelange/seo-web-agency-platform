# Prompt 19 — Brand identity development

Use when: the final business name is chosen (input #1) and before Prompt 18 (visual theme).

Context files: `docs/business/POSITIONING-STRATEGY.md`, `docs/business/TARGET-CUSTOMERS.md`, `docs/business/DIFFERENTIATION.md`, `docs/business/NAME-SHORTLIST.md`.

```
You are a brand strategist and identity designer for {{final business name}}, a South African company
positioned as: "SEO-first websites and lead-generation systems for technical, industrial and service
businesses — and the admin, quotation and portal systems behind them." Audience: owners and directors of
contractors, engineering firms, manufacturers, property and professional-services businesses (profiles
attached). They value competence, straight answers and evidence; they distrust agency gloss.

Task: develop the brand identity foundations.

Deliver:
1. Brand personality — 4–5 traits with do/don't expression examples (e.g. "precise: we show the method"
   vs "never: startup-cute copy").
2. Verbal identity — tone rules building on our editorial guidelines; tagline candidates (6–10) tested
   against the positioning sentence; elevator pitch (25 words); boilerplate paragraph (60–80 words).
3. Visual direction — 3 distinct routes (e.g. "engineering drawing precision", "confident industrial
   modernism", "premium technical minimalism"), each with: mood description, colour palette (hex, with
   WCAG contrast pre-checked for text/UI roles), typography pairing (self-hostable, WOFF2, licensing
   noted), graphic language (grids/lines/diagrams beat stock photos for this audience), what it signals
   and to whom, and one risk.
4. Logo brief — for a designer: wordmark-first requirements, clear-space, minimum sizes, mono/reverse
   variants, favicon/social-avatar behaviour. (Do not generate final logo art — brief only.)
5. Application previews — how the chosen route lands on: website header + hero, proposal/quote PDF,
   invoice, LinkedIn banner, GBP profile.
6. Recommendation — which route best matches positioning and audience, with reasoning.

Rules: nothing that visually locks us to one technology; no aesthetics that contradict the performance/
accessibility constraints in PERFORMANCE-BUDGET.md and ACCESSIBILITY-STANDARDS.md (they bind the website
expression); avoid visual clichés of the SA web-design volume market (rocket ships, gradients-on-black,
laptop stock photos).
```
